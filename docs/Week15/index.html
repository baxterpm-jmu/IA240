Week 15: Communicating Analysis and Emerging Technologies

IA 240: Technology Applications in a Networked World

This lecture combines two essential competencies for the practicing intelligence analyst: communicating quantitative findings clearly and accurately to policy consumers, and understanding the cybersecurity incidents that continue to shape the technology environment intelligence analysts work within. Communication is the terminal step in every analytical cycle — the point where the value of all prior analytical work is realized or lost.

ImportantRequired Reading Before Tuesday Class

1. Elisabeth Bumiller, “We Have Met the Enemy and He Is PowerPoint,” The New York Times (April 26, 2010).

The military and intelligence community’s PowerPoint culture — the infamous “spaghetti” Afghanistan slide, and Gen. McMaster’s warning that slides can create “the illusion of understanding.”

As you read, consider:

  • How can a briefing format make an audience feel informed while actually obscuring the underlying analysis and its uncertainty?
  • If you had to convey a complex, contested assessment without a slide deck, what would you use instead — and what would you gain or lose?

2. Brent Dykes, “Data Storytelling: The Essential Data Science Skill Everyone Needs,” Forbes (March 31, 2016).

Finding the insight is one skill; getting a decision-maker to act on it is another. The case for narrative, data, and visuals working together.

As you read, consider:

  • Dykes argues an insight is worthless if it doesn’t drive action. What turns a correct finding into one a decision-maker actually acts on?
  • Narrative can clarify but also oversimplify or mislead. Where is the line between “data storytelling” and spinning the data?

3. Josh Bernoff, “Bad Writing Is Destroying Your Company’s Productivity,” Harvard Business Review (September 6, 2016).

What unclear, bloated writing costs busy readers — and concrete habits for writing that respects the reader’s time.

As you read, consider:

  • Bernoff finds readers spend far more time reading than writers spend writing. How should that asymmetry change how you draft an intelligence product?
  • Which specific habits — structure, length, front-loading the bottom line — would make your writing cost the reader less time without sacrificing rigor?

Part I: Writing with Data

How Data-Oriented Writing Differs

TipOpening Discussion

In groups, identify at least five ways that data-oriented writing differs from qualitative or narrative-focused writing. Consider: structure, precision, evidence standards, how uncertainty is expressed, who the intended reader is, and how findings are validated.

Key dimensions of the distinction:

Qualitative/Narrative Writing

  • Evidence is textual, testimonial, or interpretive
  • Uncertainty expressed through hedge words and caveats
  • Structure tends to be inductive (evidence → conclusion)
  • Reader is asked to accept the analyst’s interpretive judgment
  • Findings are substantiated by expert authority and contextual knowledge

Data-Oriented Writing

  • Evidence is numerical and quantified
  • Uncertainty expressed through statistical measures (p-values, confidence intervals, ranges)
  • Structure tends to be deductive or IMRaD (see below)
  • Reader can evaluate and replicate the analysis
  • Findings are substantiated by transparent methodology and data access

Part II: Core Principles of Data Writing

The Five Pillars

Understand Your Audience
The complexity, terminology, and depth of statistical presentation must match the reader’s expertise. A briefing memo for a senior policymaker should not contain regression tables. A technical report for a methods specialist should not oversimplify findings. Identify your audience before writing the first sentence.
Visualize Your Data
Tables, charts, and maps are not decoration—they are evidence. Used appropriately, they communicate complex patterns more efficiently than prose. Used inappropriately, they can obscure or distort. Every visualization in a written product should carry its own descriptive caption and be referenced explicitly in the text.
Structure Your Writing (IMRaD)
Data-oriented analytical writing follows recognized structural conventions (see below). Structure signals to the reader where to find the question, the method, the data, and the findings—enabling selective reading by busy consumers.
Report Appropriate Statistical Tests
Never present a finding without the statistical evidence behind it. Report effect sizes, not just significance. Include confidence intervals. State your significance threshold.
Tell the Truth — Ethical Considerations
Data writing carries particular ethical obligations. Selective reporting, cherry-picked time periods, omitted confounders, and misleading scales are forms of analytical misconduct—even if unintentional. See Part V.

Part III: Structuring Your Writing — IMRaD

The IMRaD Framework

IMRaD (Introduction, Methodology, Results, and Discussion) is the standard structure for empirical analytical writing. A modified version for intelligence applications adds Data Description, Limitations, and Conclusions:

Introduction
Provide background context for the analysis. Present the research question or hypothesis explicitly. Justify the significance of the question—why does the answer matter, and to whom? State the scope and boundaries of the analysis (time period, geographic coverage, units of analysis).
Methodology
Describe in detail how the data was collected, cleaned, and analyzed. Explain and justify the choice of statistical methods in relation to the research question and variable types. A reader who follows your methodology description should be able to replicate your analysis.
Data Description
Present the dataset: sources, coverage period, unit of analysis, number of observations. Report descriptive statistics for key variables (mean, median, standard deviation, range, missing data rates). Describe any preprocessing steps—what was excluded and why. This section establishes that the data is fit for the analysis that follows.
Statistical Analysis Results
Present findings systematically. Use precise statistical language: report test statistics, degrees of freedom, p-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals. Use tables or charts to present results; label them clearly; reference them in the text. Do not interpret here—describe what the analysis found.
Discussion
Interpret the results in the context of the original research question. What do the findings mean? How do they relate to prior research or existing analytical assessments? What alternative explanations have been considered and ruled out? What are the implications for policy or operations?
Limitations
Acknowledge every material limitation: sample size constraints, data availability gaps, methodological simplifications, potential confounders not addressed. Be specific—vague statements like “additional research is needed” add no analytical value. Explain how limitations affect the interpretation of the findings.
Conclusions
Summarize the key findings and their implications concisely. Identify the most important takeaway for the consumer. Suggest directions for future analysis if appropriate.
NoteThe Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

In intelligence writing, the IMRaD structure is often supplemented or replaced by the BLUF format: the conclusion comes first, followed by supporting evidence and methodology. This reflects the consumer’s need to know so what before deciding whether to read the supporting detail. Write your BLUF statement first, then use it to anchor and organize the rest of the product.

Part IV: Clarity in Statistical Reporting

Contrasting Examples

Example 1 — Imprecise Reporting

After implementing changes in our customer service procedures, there appears to be a trend towards higher satisfaction in the customer surveys. The scores seem to have increased, although the exact degree of change is not clearly stated. There’s an indication that the new system might be influencing these scores positively, but the specific aspects of the survey that improved are not differentiated. The statistical analysis was performed, suggesting some level of impact from the changes, but the details of this analysis and its implications are not fully articulated.

Problems: vague hedging (“appears to be,” “seems to have,” “might be influencing”); no numbers; no statistical test reported; no differentiation across survey dimensions; reader cannot assess the evidence.

Example 2 — Precise Reporting

In our analysis of the customer satisfaction survey, we observed a significant increase in overall satisfaction scores following the implementation of the new customer service protocol. Specifically, the average satisfaction score rose from 6.5 to 8.3 on a 10-point scale, a change which was statistically significant (t(147) = 4.82, p < .001, 95% CI [1.62, 2.14]). This improvement was most pronounced in the domain of customer service responsiveness, where the satisfaction score increased by an average of 2.5 points. Our regression analysis, controlling for customer tenure, service category, and prior satisfaction, found that the new protocol contributed to approximately 60% of the observed increase in satisfaction scores (R² change = 0.23, F(1, 143) = 12.4, p < .001).

What changed: specific numbers for before/after comparison; full statistical test reported with test statistic, degrees of freedom, and p-value; confidence interval; differentiation across survey dimensions; regression analysis with controls; effect size interpretation.

Best Practices Checklist

TipStatistical Reporting Standards
  • Accuracy and precision: report exact values, not approximations; use consistent significant figures
  • Clarity: avoid jargon when writing for general audiences; define statistical terms when used
  • Consistency: use the same statistical approach and reporting format throughout a product
  • Transparency: describe your data sources, cleaning decisions, and analytical choices fully enough for replication
  • Appropriate tests: match the statistical test to the data type and research question; do not use tests that violate their assumptions
  • Limitations and uncertainties: report confidence intervals; acknowledge what you cannot rule out
  • Avoid misinterpretation: do not describe correlations as causes; do not describe statistical significance as practical significance; do not extrapolate beyond your data
  • Accessibility: present findings at a level the intended reader can process; offer supplementary technical appendices for specialists

Part V: Ethical Considerations in Data Writing

Why Ethics Matter in Data-Oriented Reporting

Data writing carries particular risks precisely because numerical precision lends false authority to claims that may rest on methodological choices, selective reporting, or omitted context. An analyst who frames their data carefully can lead a reader to a false conclusion while reporting every number accurately.

Honest Representation
Report findings fully, including results that do not support your hypothesis. Cherry-picking only confirming results is scientific misconduct whether deliberate or not. Report the full range of findings and be explicit when results are mixed or inconclusive.
Acknowledging and Addressing Bias
Identify potential sources of bias in your data collection and analysis: selection bias, survivorship bias, measurement error, researcher degrees of freedom. Do not report findings as if these limitations do not exist.
Respecting Privacy and Confidentiality
When data involves identifiable individuals, protect their privacy. Anonymize where appropriate. Consider whether the analysis could harm those whose data is included—particularly in conflict, health, or security contexts.
Proper Citation and Avoiding Plagiarism
Cite all data sources, including secondary sources for datasets. Cite the statistical methods you used if they are not standard. Do not present others’ analytical work or data as your own.
Responsibility Toward the Audience
Your analytical product will inform decisions. You have an obligation to ensure that the consumer of your product can accurately understand both the findings and their limitations. Obscuring uncertainty or overstating confidence is a failure of that obligation.
Reporting Errors and Corrections
When you discover an error in a previously disseminated product, correct it promptly and explicitly. Intelligence products that contain uncorrected errors remain in circulation and continue to influence decisions.

Part VI: Presenting Data

Core Presentation Principles

Know Your Audience

Different audiences require different presentations of the same findings:

Audience What They Need
Technical / Peer Analysts Full methodology, detailed statistical output, diagnostic checks, alternative specifications
General Public / Broad Audience Simplified key findings, accessible language, avoided jargon, relatable context
Decision-Makers Clear, actionable insights; bottom line stated upfront; implications and recommendations; limited statistical detail
WarningIn Practice: Mixed Audiences

You will often present to all three simultaneously. Structure your presentation so the bottom line is accessible to decision-makers at the start, with supporting detail available to technical audiences through backup slides, appendices, or Q&A. Know which parts you can skip if time runs short—and which are non-negotiable.

Craft a Clear Narrative

A strong analytical presentation is a story with a beginning, middle, and end:

  1. Hook: Begin with a compelling statistic, a surprising finding, or a provocative question
  2. Frame: State the research question and why it matters
  3. Method: Explain briefly how you conducted the analysis—assumptions, data, approach
  4. Findings: Walk the audience through key results in a logical sequence
  5. Conclusion: State the BLUF; identify implications; make a recommendation if appropriate

Tip: Write your BLUF before building your slides. Every slide should serve the BLUF.

Explain the Analysis

Do not assume your audience understands your methods. Provide:

  • A brief, plain-language overview of what you did and why
  • Clear statement of key assumptions and their implications
  • An example of how a result is interpreted (e.g., “A coefficient of 0.4 means that for every additional $1 billion in military spending, the probability of conflict onset increases by approximately 4 percentage points”)

Interpret the Results

Do not stop at describing findings—interpret them:

  • Statistical significance: p-values, confidence intervals; what can and cannot be ruled out
  • Practical significance: Is the effect substantively large or trivially small? An effect can be statistically significant and analytically irrelevant.
  • Trends and patterns: What do the numbers mean for the analytical question?

Formatting Tips for Slides

  • Limit text — bullet points and short phrases only; full sentences belong in the speaker notes
  • One visualization per slide — do not crowd multiple charts onto one slide
  • Clean, consistent design — uniform fonts, colors, and layout; remove decorative elements
  • Descriptive headers — the reader should understand where you are going from the slide title alone. Test: if you removed everything except the title, could an informed reader predict what the slide says?
  • Proofread — errors on slides undermine your credibility immediately
  • Prepare backup slides — anticipate questions; have ready answers supported by additional data

Engaging the Audience

Techniques for Engagement

  • Tell a story: frame your data in a real-world scenario with human stakes
  • Start with a hook: a striking statistic, a counterintuitive finding, or a question the audience cannot immediately answer
  • Use analogies and relatable examples to make abstract findings concrete
  • Pause for questions—and actively invite participation
  • End with a memorable takeaway: one sentence the audience will remember

Personal Recommendations

You will often know more about your topic than anyone else in the room. Have confidence in that knowledge.

It is acceptable—and professionally appropriate—to say: “I don’t know, but I will find out.” Never fabricate or bluff.

Actually answer the question asked. If you cannot, use the line above.

Practice. Practice enough that if you had no slides, no technology, or half the time allotted, you could still deliver the essential findings. Practice standing up, in front of at least one other person, under conditions resembling the actual presentation.

Part VII: Intelligence Writing Products — A Taxonomy

What Gets Written in the IC

Academic analytical writing and intelligence writing share underlying logical structures (claim, evidence, reasoning) but differ significantly in format, length, classification, and consumer relationship. Understanding the major intelligence product types helps contextualize the writing principles discussed in this lecture.

President’s Daily Brief (PDB)
The most senior finished intelligence product in the U.S. system. Produced by the Office of the Director of National Intelligence; delivered to the President and a small number of senior officials. Typically 1–2 pages per article; BLUF format; almost no statistical detail. The consumer has thirty seconds to absorb the key point. The PDB represents the most extreme version of the audience-calibrated brevity principle.
Intelligence Information Report (IIR)
Formal reporting of single-source HUMINT or SIGINT collection. Structured format: source description (without naming the source), information report, reliability and credibility assessment. IIRs are the raw intelligence that analysts use to build assessments; they are not themselves finished intelligence products.
Finished Intelligence Assessment
Analytical products that interpret, integrate, and contextualize information from multiple sources. Can range from 2–3 page memos to 20+ page formal assessments. Include key judgments up front (BLUF), source assessment, analytical confidence level, and an identified analytic line with supporting evidence.
National Intelligence Estimate (NIE)
The IC’s highest-confidence, most formally reviewed analytical product. Produced under the aegis of the National Intelligence Council with input from across the community. Includes key judgments, supporting analysis, dissents, and confidence-level indicators. The 2002 Iraq WMD NIE is the most-studied example — and the most cautionary.
Signals Intelligence Report (SIGINT Report)
Formatted product reporting technical collection (electronic, communications intelligence). Strict formatting requirements govern source protection — original intercept content is paraphrased rather than directly quoted to protect collection methods.
Watch Officer / Duty Officer Report
Brief operational reports on time-sensitive developments, produced on short timelines (minutes to hours). The premium is on accuracy, timeliness, and immediate actionability — not analytical depth.

Part VIII: Analytical Standards and Confidence Language

Intelligence Community Directive 203 — Analytic Standards

ICD 203 sets the analytic standards that govern all finished intelligence products across the U.S. intelligence community. The core standards are:

Objectivity
Analysts must assess issues free from political considerations. Intelligence should not be produced to support a predetermined policy conclusion — the fundamental failure mode of the 2002 Iraq WMD assessment, where analysts faced pressure to “connect the dots” in a particular direction.
Independent of Political Considerations
Closely related to objectivity but distinct: even well-intentioned analysts can allow awareness of policymaker preferences to shape their conclusions without deliberate bias. ICD 203 requires structural separation of intelligence production from policy advocacy.
Timeliness
Intelligence has no value after the decision it was meant to inform. The standard requires that products be delivered in time to be actionable — which imposes real tradeoffs between thoroughness and speed.
Proper Sourcing
Every key judgment must be traceable to source reporting. Analytical characterizations of source reliability and collection method must accompany finished intelligence so consumers can assess the evidentiary foundation.
Acknowledgment of Uncertainty
Analysts must explicitly state what they do not know, what is assumed rather than evidenced, and how alternative interpretations of the evidence would change the conclusion.
Distinction Between Assessment and Fact
Intelligence products must clearly distinguish factual reporting (what sources said) from analytical judgments (what analysts believe it means). The reader must always be able to tell the difference.

Confidence Levels and Uncertainty Language

ICD 203 also standardizes the language analysts use to express confidence in their judgments. This is the intelligence community’s version of the statistical confidence interval:

Confidence Level Meaning Typical Usage
High Confidence The assessment is based on high-quality information and/or the nature of the issue makes it possible to render a solid judgment. “We assess with high confidence that…”
Moderate Confidence The information is credible and plausible but not of sufficient quality or corroboration to warrant a higher level of confidence. “We assess with moderate confidence that…”
Low Confidence The information is fragmentary, of questionable reliability, or the issue is inherently difficult to assess. “We assess with low confidence that…”

Probability language: ICD 203 also standardizes probabilistic language to reduce consumer misinterpretation of terms like “likely,” “probably,” and “possibly”:

Term Approximate Probability
Almost certainly / Almost certainly not >95% / <5%
Very likely / Very unlikely ~85–95% / ~5–15%
Likely / Unlikely ~55–85% / ~15–45%
Roughly even chance ~45–55%
Possibly <55% (used when probability is uncertain)
WarningThe 2002 NIE and Confidence Inflation

A retrospective study of the 2002 Iraq NIE found that “high confidence” was assigned to key judgments that rested on thin, single-source reporting and circular source evaluation (sources that appeared to corroborate each other actually derived from the same original report). The confidence language gave consumers an accuracy impression the underlying evidence did not support. Properly calibrated confidence language requires honest assessment of source quality and corroboration, not consistency with the desired conclusion.

Part IX: Source Protection in Written Products

Protecting Methods While Communicating Findings

A persistent tension in intelligence writing is between being specific enough to be convincing and protecting the sources and methods that generated the evidence. Key principles:

  • Never identify a source by characteristics that could enable hostile identification. A HUMINT source described as “a senior official with access to the defense ministry” is potentially identifiable to a counterintelligence service. Standard practice is to describe access, not identity.

  • Paraphrase, do not directly quote, SIGINT-derived reporting unless the product has undergone formal sanitization review. Direct quotation may expose collection techniques, language processing methods, or implant capabilities.

  • Compartmentalization of source information. The sourcing appendices of senior finished intelligence products are often classified at a higher level than the assessment itself — allowing wider dissemination of the analytical judgments without revealing the source base.

  • “We assess” vs. “Source reports.” The linguistic convention of “we assess” signals an analytical judgment; “source reports” or “reporting indicates” signals factual collection. This distinction should always be maintained in writing.

Part X: Computing and National Security — Historical Cases

Case 1: The Morris Worm (1988)

On November 2, 1988, Cornell graduate student Robert Morris released a self-replicating program onto the ARPANET. Within 24 hours, an estimated 6,000 computers — roughly 10% of the Internet at the time — had been infected and rendered largely inoperable.

NoteThe Dual-Use Problem

Morris was prosecuted under the 1986 Computer Fraud and Abuse Act — the first such conviction. The case illustrates a pattern in computing history: tools developed for legitimate research regularly find their way into offensive use, often before defensive measures exist.

Case 2: Stuxnet (2009–2010)

Stuxnet was a sophisticated piece of malware discovered in 2010 that had been silently sabotaging Iran’s Natanz uranium enrichment facility since approximately 2009. It targeted Siemens industrial controllers that managed centrifuge speeds, causing centrifuges to spin at damaging speeds while reporting normal operation to Iranian operators.

Using Libicki’s three-layer framework:

Physical Layer The centrifuges and industrial control systems at Natanz — physically separate from the Internet (an “air-gapped” network). Stuxnet was introduced via infected USB drives.

Syntactic Layer The malware itself: four zero-day exploits, two stolen legitimate digital certificates, a rootkit that hid its presence, and a PLC payload that modified centrifuge operation while spoofing sensor readings.

Semantic Layer Iranian operators saw normal readings. The gap between reality (centrifuges failing) and displayed information (normal) was the attack’s most sophisticated element — exploiting human trust in instrumentation.

Intelligence takeaway: The most consequential element of Stuxnet was not the code — it was the deception. The syntactic layer enabled physical destruction; the semantic layer prevented detection.

WarningAttribution and Escalation

Stuxnet is widely attributed to the United States and Israel (Operation Olympic Games), though neither government has officially confirmed this. The case raised fundamental questions: Does a cyberattack on industrial infrastructure constitute an act of war? What are the rules of engagement for offensive cyber operations?

Case 3: BGP Hijacking and Routing Vulnerabilities

The Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) is the routing protocol that determines how Internet traffic moves between autonomous systems. It was designed for trust, not security: routers accept routing announcements without verification.

Year Actor What Happened
2008 Pakistan Telecom Accidentally announced routes for YouTube’s IP space, taking the site offline globally for ~2 hours
2010 China Telecom Briefly rerouted ~15% of global Internet traffic — including U.S. government and military traffic — through Chinese infrastructure
2017 Rostelecom (Russia) Hijacked traffic to Google, Apple, Facebook, and Microsoft for ~80 minutes
2018 Multiple actors BGP routes for Amazon Route 53 DNS hijacked to redirect cryptocurrency wallet traffic to attacker-controlled servers

BGP hijacking can be accidental (misconfiguration) or deliberate (man-in-the-middle interception). Because BGP lacks cryptographic authentication, routing announcements from any peer are accepted as legitimate.
