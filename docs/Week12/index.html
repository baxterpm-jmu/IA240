Week 12: Geospatial and GEOINT Analysis

IA 240: Technology Applications in a Networked World

Geospatial intelligence (GEOINT) integrates imagery analysis, geographic information systems (GIS), and spatial statistics to produce intelligence from the physical world. The commercialization of satellite imagery has simultaneously democratized GEOINT and raised new questions about open-source collection, verification, and the limits of overhead analysis. This lecture covers imagery interpretation methodology, GIS tools, spatial statistical methods, and the major contemporary applications and case studies.

ImportantRequired Reading Before Tuesday Class

Alison Killing, Megha Rajagopalan & Christo Buschek, “Blanked-Out Spots on China’s Maps Helped Us Uncover Xinjiang’s Camps,” BuzzFeed News (August 27, 2020).

A Pulitzer-winning investigation that located more than 260 detention sites in Xinjiang using public satellite imagery — starting from the “blank” tiles China had censored on its own mapping platforms, then corroborating with former detainees.

As you read, consider:

  • The team turned an absence of data (censored map tiles) into a lead. How does “what’s deliberately missing” itself become intelligence?
  • The findings relied on satellite imagery cross-checked against interviews. Why is corroboration across independent sources essential before trusting overhead imagery alone?

Part I: What is GEOINT?

Defining Geospatial Intelligence

GEOINT is the process of collecting, analyzing, and using geospatial information to produce intelligence products. It integrates imagery, geospatial data, and analytic techniques to generate actionable insights that help decision-makers understand and monitor physical locations, activities, and patterns on Earth.

GEOINT rests on three pillars:

Imagery

Visual data from satellites, drones, manned aircraft, and ground-based sensors that capture the Earth’s surface and activities on it. Includes electro-optical (EO), infrared (IR), multispectral, hyperspectral, and radar imagery.

Geospatial Data

Geographic information about natural and man-made features and their spatial relationships: maps, terrain elevation models, road networks, population distributions, administrative boundaries.

Analytic Processes

Methods for interpreting imagery and geospatial data to produce intelligence products. This encompasses imagery analysis, spatial statistics, change detection, pattern recognition, and predictive modeling.

NoteGEOINT ≠ IMINT

IMINT refers specifically to imagery exploitation. GEOINT is a broader discipline that includes IMINT but also incorporates geospatial data, GIS analysis, and spatial modeling. GIS enables GEOINT; GEOINT is not GIS.

Part II: Imagery Analysis

What Imagery Analysis Involves

Imagery analysis is the systematic examination of visual data—satellite photographs, aerial imagery, radar returns—to extract intelligence. Key tasks:

Feature Identification
Recognizing and locating specific objects or phenomena within an image: buildings, vehicles, vessels, infrastructure, vegetation type. Requires knowledge of the appearance of objects at different resolutions, angles, and seasons.
Change Detection
Comparing imagery from different time periods to identify what has changed: new construction, deforestation, movement of military equipment, changes in irrigation patterns. Change detection is the foundation of monitoring programs.
Pattern Recognition
Identifying regular, recurring formations or activities that suggest systematic behavior. Examples: distinguishing legitimate fishing fleets from illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing by vessel pattern and transponder behavior; identifying industrial waste dumping from drainage patterns.
Spectral Analysis
Evaluating the reflectance or emission properties of surface features across multiple wavelength bands (multispectral/hyperspectral). Healthy vegetation has a distinctive spectral signature that differs from stressed vegetation, bare soil, or water—enabling discrimination invisible to the naked eye.
Photogrammetry and Measurement
Using the geometry of imagery to derive metric information: building heights from shadow lengths, storage tank capacity from diameter and roof height, vehicle dimensions for type identification.

Part III: Geographic Information Systems (GIS)

What is GIS?

A Geographic Information System is a framework for capturing, managing, analyzing, and displaying all forms of geographically referenced information. GIS enables analysts to:

  • Integrate layers of spatial data (terrain, infrastructure, population, weather)
  • Perform spatial queries: “Show all facilities within 50km of this border crossing”
  • Apply statistical analysis to spatially distributed data
  • Produce maps, models, and visualizations that communicate spatial patterns

GIS enables GEOINT; GEOINT is not reducible to GIS.

Broad applications: urban planning, environmental monitoring, transportation, public health (disease mapping), agriculture (precision farming), disaster management, and military operations.

Part IV: A Process for Imagery Interpretation

Analytical Methodology

The following steps describe a rigorous approach to imagery interpretation—applicable to satellite imagery, aerial photographs, or any visual intelligence product:

Step 1: Where do I look?
Establish the geographic area of interest (AOI) based on the analytical question. Determine relevant spatial context: what should be nearby if the hypothesis is correct? Reference maps, prior imagery, and geospatial data to orient yourself.
Step 2: What am I looking at?
Initial assessment of the scene. Identify land cover types, infrastructure, and major features. Establish what is expected to be present based on geospatial context and prior knowledge.
Step 3: Look closely at details
Zoom in systematically. Small details—vehicle tracks, construction debris, new fencing, temporary structures, exhaust stains—often carry more intelligence value than large-scale features. Resist the tendency to categorize quickly.
Step 4: Look at developments over time
Compare current imagery with historical imagery. What has changed? When did it change? The rate of change can be as informative as the change itself. Rapid construction often signals urgency.
Step 5: Apply measurement and technical techniques
Measure structures, shadows, vehicles, and infrastructure using photogrammetric methods. Measure storage capacity. Apply spectral analysis if multispectral data is available.
Step 6: Look at what should be there
If this is what you think it is, what else should you expect to see? A plutonium production reactor requires cooling water, electrical power, ventilation, security infrastructure, and transportation access. Absence of expected features is evidence too.
Step 7: Investigate what you don’t know
Research technical specifications, construction standards, and comparable facilities. Consult domain experts. Use open-source references to understand what you are looking at.
Step 8: Validate with other evidence
No imagery finding should stand alone. Cross-check with SIGINT, HUMINT, OSINT, and other GEOINT to confirm or challenge the interpretation.
Step 9: Confirm with others
Have a colleague examine the same imagery independently. Seek review from technical specialists when the interpretation requires specialized knowledge. Document dissenting views.

Part V: Case Study — Pakistan’s Khushab Reactors

Assessing Nuclear Capability from Satellite Imagery

One of the more challenging tasks in GEOINT is assessing the capacity of facilities whose function cannot be directly observed. A notable example: estimating the plutonium production capability of Pakistan’s Khushab reactor complex.1

The challenge: Experts disagreed sharply on reactor capacity, with estimates ranging from 40 MWt to 1,000 MWt. The range was so wide as to render policy conclusions nearly meaningless.

The approach: Patton used satellite imagery of the cooling towers combined with 3D facility modeling and thermodynamic principles. Cooling tower dimensions constrain the heat load they can dissipate, which is directly related to reactor thermal output.

The findings: - Khushab II: estimated at 58–73 MWt - Khushab III: estimated at 72–90 MWt

These estimates dramatically narrowed the policy-relevant uncertainty range using only open-source imagery and physics-based reasoning—no classified collection required.

NoteKey Principle: Facility Modeling

Physical constraints impose limits on what a facility can be or do. A cooling tower of given dimensions cannot dissipate more than a certain heat load. A storage tank of given dimensions cannot hold more than a certain volume. Building dimensions constrain interpretations. This is the logic of facility modeling: use what you can measure to constrain what you cannot observe directly.

Spatial Statistics Tools

Pattern Analysis:

  • Hot Spot Analysis (Getis-Ord Gi*): Identifies statistically significant clusters of high values (hot spots) and low values (cold spots). For each feature, computes a Z-score comparing its value and its neighbors’ values to the global mean. Applications: IED emplacement patterns, conflict event clustering, disease outbreak mapping.

  • Cluster and Outlier Analysis (Anselin Local Moran’s I): Categorizes each feature into one of five spatial relationship types: High-High (HH), Low-Low (LL), High-Low (HL), Low-High (LH), or Not Significant. Useful for identifying anomalous features embedded in otherwise uniform landscapes.

  • Kernel Density: Estimates the density of point features across a continuous surface, producing a smooth density raster. Applications: visualizing concentration of activity in an operational area.

Spatial Autocorrelation:

  • Global Moran’s I: Tests whether similar values cluster spatially across the entire dataset (positive autocorrelation) or are dispersed (negative autocorrelation). A prerequisite check before many spatial statistical analyses.

Part X: The Commercial Satellite Revolution

From Classified to Open: GEOINT Goes Democratic

For most of the Cold War, high-resolution satellite imagery was the exclusive province of national technical means (NTM) — classified satellite systems operated by the United States (KH-series, later CORONA and successors) and the Soviet Union. Imagery was closely guarded and rarely shared even among allied intelligence services.

This has fundamentally changed. The commercial satellite industry now operates hundreds of imaging satellites that provide sub-meter resolution imagery to paying customers worldwide, including academic researchers, journalists, NGOs, and foreign governments. This transformation has profound implications for intelligence analysis.

Key commercial satellite providers:

Provider Notable Systems Resolution Revisit Rate
Maxar Technologies WorldView-3, WorldView-4, GeoEye-1 30 cm panchromatic 1 day (at mid-latitudes)
Planet Labs Dove constellation (200+ satellites), SkySat 3–5 m (Dove), 50 cm (SkySat) Daily full-Earth coverage (Dove)
Airbus Defence & Space Pleiades, Pleiades Neo 30 cm 1 day
Capella Space SAR satellites 50 cm (SAR) Day/night, all-weather
Satellogic High-cadence optical 70 cm Sub-daily
NoteWhy SAR Matters

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) satellites transmit their own microwave radar pulses and measure the return signal. Unlike electro-optical imagery, SAR does not require sunlight and penetrates cloud cover. This makes SAR invaluable for monitoring areas with persistent cloud cover (tropical regions, Arctic conditions) or for time-sensitive military monitoring where nighttime collection is required.

What Commercial GEOINT Has Enabled: Open-Source Intelligence Operations

The democratization of imagery has enabled non-governmental organizations, journalists, and academic researchers to conduct satellite-based investigations that previously required government-level access.

Part XI: Real-World Case Studies

Case 1: Xinjiang Detention Facilities — Open-Source GEOINT at Scale

Beginning in 2017, journalists and researchers began receiving accounts of mass detention of Uyghurs and other Muslim minorities in China’s Xinjiang region. The Chinese government denied the existence of the facilities or characterized them as voluntary “vocational training centers.”

The GEOINT investigation:

Researchers at the Australian Strategic Policy Institute (ASPI) and BuzzFeed News developed a methodology to identify, locate, and characterize suspected detention facilities using:

  1. Change detection: Comparing imagery from 2016–2017 (pre-expansion) to 2018–2020 (post-expansion) to identify new large-scale construction in previously undeveloped areas
  2. Facility signature identification: Guard towers, double perimeter fencing, internal spatial division consistent with detention architecture, exercise yards, and administrative buildings
  3. Corroboration with leaked documents: The “China Cables” and “Xinjiang Papers” (2019) provided internal Chinese government documentation that could be cross-referenced with the imagery findings
  4. Measurement: Shadow-based height estimates of guard towers; perimeter wall length and construction quality estimation

Findings: Over 380 suspected detention sites identified across Xinjiang, with a cumulative capacity estimated at approximately 1 million detainees. The spatial distribution of facilities corresponded with areas of highest Uyghur population concentration.

WarningLimitations and Counter-Arguments

The Chinese government disputed both the detention characterization and the capacity estimates. Imagery analysis can confirm that a facility exists and that it has detention-consistent architectural features — it cannot directly confirm what occurs inside. The investigation required corroboration from documentary leaks, survivor testimony, and procurement records to reach its conclusions. No single source was sufficient.

Case 2: Russian Military Buildup 2021–22

In the fall and winter of 2021–22, commercial satellite providers — particularly Maxar Technologies — documented an unprecedented massing of Russian military forces along the Ukrainian border. This imagery was declassified and publicly shared by U.S. officials and media organizations, creating an unusual intelligence transparency initiative.

What imagery showed:

October–November 2021

  • New armored vehicle deployments to Yelnya (450 km from Ukraine)
  • Artillery systems and logistics convoys
  • Field maintenance and staging activity
  • Temporary field hospitals

Key indicator: The combination of combat systems + logistics + medical capacity at field staging areas — not merely the combat systems alone

January–February 2022

  • Forces moved forward from staging areas to jump-off positions
  • Bridging equipment deployed near river crossing points
  • Helicopter units repositioned to forward air bases
  • Ammunition and fuel pre-positioned at forward logistics bases

Key indicator: The transition from staging to pre-attack positioning: forces moving from sustainable garrison posture to unsustainable forward deployment requiring action or withdrawal

The intelligence transparency dimension: The U.S. government’s decision to publicly release intelligence (including satellite imagery) before the invasion was a deliberate policy choice to deny Russia the element of surprise, complicate its information operation (which denied invasion intent), and build allied coalition cohesion. This represented a novel use of commercial GEOINT as a diplomatic and deterrence instrument.

Case 3: Monitoring North Korea’s Nuclear and Missile Programs — 38 North

The 38 North project at the Stimson Center has maintained continuous open-source GEOINT monitoring of North Korea’s nuclear and missile infrastructure since 2009. The North Korean case is analytically important because almost no HUMINT source access exists and most SIGINT is inaccessible. GEOINT is one of the few available collection methods.

What has been revealed through commercial imagery:

  • Expansion of uranium enrichment halls at Yongbyon (2010–present)
  • Construction and testing activity at the Sohae Satellite Launching Station
  • Submarine construction at Sinpo shipyard (ongoing monitoring of SLBM-capable submarines)
  • Rocket engine test stand activity at facilities linked to ICBM development
  • Evidence of activity consistent with plutonium reprocessing operations

The shadow measurement technique at Yongbyon: The Khushab case study in Part V illustrates the general technique. At Yongbyon, analysts have used: - Smoke stack emissions height estimation (indicates operational vs. inactive status) - Vehicle and crane presence patterns (construction or maintenance activity) - Snow melt patterns around reactor buildings (warm coolant outflow indicating reactor operation) - Water vapor plumes from cooling towers (operational signatures)

These physical indicators — none of which directly observes the classified process occurring inside the facility — together constitute a compelling composite picture of operational status.

Part XII: Counter-GEOINT and Denial and Deception

Adversary Countermeasures to Overhead Imagery

Sophisticated state actors are aware that their facilities are observed by satellite and take active measures to complicate imagery analysis.

Camouflage and Concealment
Physical measures to make facilities appear to be something other than what they are, or to blend into the background. Examples: painting building roofs to blend with surrounding terrain; covering equipment with nets; constructing false rooftop features.
Covered Operations
Building covered structures (hangars, tunnels, underground facilities) to deny overhead observation of sensitive activities. North Korea has extensively tunneled its military and nuclear infrastructure; Iran constructs enrichment and missile facilities partially or entirely underground.
Scheduling Around Satellite Passes
When satellite pass times are predictable, adversaries can schedule sensitive activities between passes or during periods of cloud cover. Reducing revisit time (more frequent collections) and using SAR (which penetrates cloud) partially counters this technique.
Deception Structures
Constructing facilities that mimic the appearance of legitimate civilian or military uses while concealing their actual function. May include creating “dummy” facilities to draw collection attention away from actual sites.
Pattern of Life Disruption
Deliberately altering normal activity patterns to complicate change detection and pattern-of-life analysis. If normal activity resumes, analysts may discount genuine anomalies as within-baseline variation.
WarningThe Analyst’s Response: Multi-Source Corroboration

No single denial and deception technique is effective against multi-source collection. Camouflage that deceives optical imagery does not defeat thermal IR (which detects heat signatures) or SAR (which responds to surface roughness and structure). Underground facilities can be detected by seismic monitoring, airborne sampling, and supply chain tracking. The intelligence response to D&D is multi-INT collection and structured analysis that asks: what should be present here if this is what it appears to be — and why isn’t it?
