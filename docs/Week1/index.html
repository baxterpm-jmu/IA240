Week 1: Technology and the Intelligence Cycle

IA 240: Technology Applications in a Networked World

In this course, students are introduced to a broad array of technical concepts and applications, such as data structure, databases, statistical models, and computational tools, that apply and advance their analytical skills. You will gain an appreciation of the technical analysis landscape, models, and best practices in exploring and assessing a variety of data types. This hands-on course supports the development of working technical knowledge in accessing, evaluating, modeling, and analyzing information and data for problem-solving. This lecture sets the stage by examining how the data-rich environment intelligence analysts work in today evolved, how that evolution impacts the role of an analyst today, and where this fits within the intelligence cycle.

Part I: A Brief History of Computing

Why History Matters for Analysts

Understanding how computing evolved is not merely trivia—it shapes how we understand the current technical infrastructure that intelligence, communications, and commerce depend on today. Every vulnerability, every collection opportunity, and every analytical tool available today has roots in decades of design decisions made by engineers and governments.

Key Milestones

1942 — Atanasoff-Berry Computer (ABC)
The first electronic digital computer, designed by John Atanasoff and Clifford Berry at Iowa State. It solved linear equations using binary arithmetic and electronic switches—foundational concepts still in use today. It was never programmable, but it proved that electronic computation was feasible.
1945 — ENIAC
The Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer, built at the University of Pennsylvania. ENIAC was the first general-purpose electronic computer—programmable, though reconfiguring it required physically rewiring cables. It performed ballistics calculations for the U.S. Army and demonstrated that computing could solve real national security problems.
1948 — Manchester Baby (SSEM)
The Small-Scale Experimental Machine, the first computer to store its program in electronic memory (RAM). This “stored program” concept—where instructions and data occupy the same memory—is the architecture underlying virtually every modern computer.
1953 — LEO I
Lyons Electronic Office, the world’s first business computer. Operated by J. Lyons & Co. in the UK to handle payroll and inventory, LEO demonstrated that computing had commercial applications far beyond scientific calculation.
1969 — ARPANET
Funded by the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), ARPANET was the first packet-switched wide-area network. It initially connected four university nodes and laid the architectural and conceptual groundwork for the modern Internet.
1981 — IBM Personal Computer
IBM’s entry into the personal computer market legitimized the PC as a business tool and established the x86 architecture and open hardware standards that still dominate today. Within years, millions of Americans had computing power once reserved for institutions.
1983 — TCP/IP becomes the standard
On January 1, 1983 (“Flag Day”), ARPANET formally adopted TCP/IP as its networking protocol, replacing the earlier NCP. This transition created the technical foundation for the modern Internet.
1984 — Apple Macintosh
The Macintosh introduced the graphical user interface (GUI) to mass consumers, making computing accessible without programming knowledge. It transformed how humans interact with machines.
1985 — Microsoft Windows
Microsoft’s GUI operating system extended the accessibility of personal computing to IBM-compatible PCs, eventually dominating the enterprise computing market.
1989 — World Wide Web / Open Internet
Tim Berners-Lee proposed the World Wide Web at CERN as a hypertext-based information system. By 1991, the first website was live. The web transformed the Internet from a research network into a global public infrastructure.

Part II: Internet Structure and Infrastructure

How the Internet Works

The Internet is a physical infrastructure—cables (including undersea fiber-optic cables carrying ~95% of intercontinental data), routers, data centers, and the computers that connect to them. It is not “the cloud”—it is very much a physical entity with geography and jurisdiction.

Internet Exchange Points (IXPs)

Internet Exchange Points are the hubs where different networks (ISPs, CDNs, corporations) physically connect to exchange traffic. They are critical infrastructure: disrupting or monitoring a major IXP has outsized effects on regional or global Internet traffic.

NoteIntelligence Significance of Physical Infrastructure

Geography matters in cyberspace. Approximately 25% of global Internet traffic routes through Bude, Cornwall, UK—where undersea cables from North America land. GCHQ’s TEMPORA program historically tapped these cables to split and copy the data flow. When analysts say “location of servers matters,” they mean that jurisdiction determines what data can be legally collected, retained, and shared.

Case Study: Baltic Sea Cable Severances (2023–2024)

Between October 2023 and December 2024, at least six undersea cables and one natural gas pipeline were severed in the Baltic Sea under circumstances suggesting deliberate targeting rather than accidental damage. The incidents — each attributed by investigators to a vessel dragging its anchor across the seabed — collectively disrupted telecommunications and energy infrastructure linking NATO members Finland, Estonia, Sweden, Germany, and Lithuania.

The pattern unfolded in three phases. In October 2023, the Balticconnector gas pipeline connecting Finland and Estonia and a parallel data cable were severed. Finnish investigators identified the Chinese-flagged cargo ship New Polar Bear as the likely cause; the vessel transited the damage site but did not stop or report an incident. In November 2024, the C-Lion1 fiber cable connecting Germany and Finland and the BCS East-West Interlink cable between Sweden and Lithuania were cut within hours of each other. Finnish and Swedish coast guards monitored the Chinese bulk carrier Yi Peng 3, which anchored in the Kattegat strait for weeks while Swedish authorities sought boarding permission. In December 2024, the Estlink-2 high-voltage power cable between Finland and Estonia — one of the region’s critical electricity transmission links — was severed. Finnish authorities intercepted and seized the Russian-flagged oil tanker Eagle S, part of Russia’s so-called “shadow fleet” of vessels operating outside Western sanctions.

WarningThe Attribution Problem in Infrastructure Attacks

None of the three vessel incidents produced a confirmed criminal conviction or definitive state attribution during the period of documented damage. This illustrates a core challenge in gray-zone infrastructure attacks: the physical evidence (a dragged anchor, a damaged cable) is consistent with both accident and deliberate sabotage. States can plausibly deny intent. Third-country vessels — Chinese-flagged ships in the 2023 and early 2024 incidents, a Russian shadow-fleet tanker in the December case — add a layer of deniability even when the geopolitical context makes deliberate action a reasonable hypothesis.

For intelligence analysts, the incidents demonstrate that physical infrastructure attacks need not be dramatic to be consequential. A ship moving at three knots, dragging an anchor across 100 meters of seabed, can sever a cable carrying terabits of data per second — and do so in a manner that lawyers argue is indistinguishable from negligence.

The Baltic incidents accelerated NATO’s focus on undersea critical infrastructure protection, produced new multi-nation naval patrol frameworks for the Baltic, and put undersea cable geography squarely into strategic planning conversations that previously treated cables as a logistics issue rather than a security one.

Case Study: Russian Cyber Operations Against Estonia (2007) and Georgia (2008)

Russia launched significant cyber operations against both Estonia and Georgia within fourteen months of each other — but the two campaigns produced strikingly different outcomes. The divergence is instructive for understanding how context, infrastructure resilience, and operational coordination shape the actual effects of cyberattacks.

Estonia, April–May 2007

Following Estonia’s decision to relocate a Soviet-era war memorial (the “Bronze Soldier”) in Tallinn, a wave of distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks struck Estonian government websites, banks, news media, and telecommunications providers. The attacks lasted approximately three weeks, peaking in the first days of May.

Effects were real but manageable: Estonian government sites were intermittently inaccessible, the largest commercial bank suspended online services for roughly 90 minutes, and Estonian media struggled to maintain online presence. No physical systems were destroyed, no data was exfiltrated, and critical national services continued.

Estonia recovered relatively quickly for several reasons. The country had built one of the world’s most mature e-government systems — with deliberate redundancy — and had invested heavily in cybersecurity capacity. Estonian technical experts recognized the attack pattern early, implemented traffic-filtering countermeasures, and called in international assistance from Finland, Germany, Slovenia, and the United States. Estonia also escalated immediately through NATO, framing the attacks as an act of collective concern. The result was the establishment of NATO’s Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre of Excellence (CCDCOE) in Tallinn in 2008 — a permanent institutional response to a three-week DDoS campaign.

Georgia, August 2008

The Russian cyber campaign against Georgia began approximately two weeks before Russian tanks crossed into South Ossetia — and continued throughout the five-day conventional war. This temporal and operational integration with kinetic military action was the defining difference from Estonia.

Georgian government and media websites were taken offline during the period when the Georgian government most needed to communicate domestically and internationally. President Saakashvili’s official website — the primary channel for the government’s narrative about Russian military operations — was defaced and intermittently unreachable. The Ministry of Foreign Affairs temporarily migrated its website to a Blogspot server hosted in the United States (Georgia the U.S. state). Georgian television could not broadcast updates from the front.

The effects were strategically more significant than Estonia’s, not because the technical attacks were more sophisticated (they were not — both campaigns relied primarily on DDoS), but because Georgia faced them under three compounding conditions Estonia did not: active simultaneous military attack, significantly less developed digital infrastructure and response capacity, and no NATO membership to provide rapid allied assistance.

Why the Outcomes Diverged

The Estonia–Georgia comparison is a canonical case for understanding that the severity of a cyberattack is not determined solely by the technical sophistication of the attack. Four structural factors drove the difference:

Integration with kinetic operations
The Georgia attacks were designed to degrade communications during a shooting war, creating a force-multiplier effect that DDoS attacks on a peacetime government cannot replicate. Disrupting a foreign ministry website for 90 minutes is an embarrassment; disrupting it while your territory is being invaded is a strategic communications failure.
Infrastructure maturity and resilience
Estonia’s e-government systems had been built with distributed architecture and redundancy; Georgia’s had not. The same attack volume produced different network effects because the target architectures differed fundamentally.
Institutional capacity to respond
Estonia mobilized a trained technical response within hours and had pre-existing relationships with international partners to call on. Georgia’s cyber response capacity was overwhelmed, and its government was simultaneously managing a military crisis.
Alliance structures and solidarity
NATO membership meant Estonia had immediate recourse to allied assistance and political framing. Georgia’s request for NATO assistance in 2008 produced sympathy but no Article 5 mechanism to invoke.
NoteAnalytical Takeaway

Both campaigns illustrate that infrastructure vulnerability is not solely a technical property — it is a function of the interaction between attacker capabilities, defender resilience, operational context, and alliance support. An analyst assessing cyber risk to a state cannot simply catalog that state’s technical vulnerabilities; they must also assess the political and military context in which a potential attack would occur.

TCP/IP: The Internet’s Foundation

TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol) defines how data is packaged, addressed, transmitted, and received across the Internet. It operates across five conceptual layers:

Layer Name Function
1 Physical / Data Transport Bits on physical media (cables, radio)
2 Data Link Node-to-node data transfer, MAC addressing
3 Network (IP) Routing packets across networks using IP addresses
4 Transport (TCP/UDP) End-to-end connection management, error correction
5 Application User-facing protocols: HTTP, SMTP, DNS, FTP

The key concept is packet switching: data is broken into packets, each routed independently across the network, and reassembled at the destination. This design made the Internet resilient—there is no single point of failure—but it also distributes data in ways that can be intercepted at any routing node.

Case Study: BGP Hijacking and Routing Vulnerabilities

TCP/IP’s packet-switching design achieves resilience through decentralization — no central authority determines how packets travel from source to destination. Instead, autonomous systems (AS), the networks operated by ISPs, corporations, and governments, advertise reachability information to each other using the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP). BGP tells the Internet’s routers which AS can reach which IP addresses. The problem is that BGP was designed in an era when Internet participants were assumed to be cooperative, so it has no cryptographic authentication mechanism. Any AS can announce that it owns IP address blocks it does not own, and other routers will generally believe the claim and reroute traffic accordingly.

This is BGP hijacking — one of the most consequential and underappreciated vulnerabilities in Internet infrastructure.

Pakistan Telecom, February 2008

Pakistan Telecommunications Company (PTC) attempted to block YouTube within Pakistan by advertising internally that PTC owned YouTube’s IP address block. Due to a misconfiguration, that announcement leaked to PTC’s upstream provider and propagated globally. For approximately two hours, YouTube traffic worldwide was rerouted to Pakistan, where PTC had no YouTube content to serve — effectively taking YouTube offline globally. The incident was almost certainly accidental, but it illustrated that a single autonomous system could disrupt a major platform for the entire Internet.

China Telecom, April 2010

For approximately 18 minutes, China Telecom advertised routes for roughly 50,000 IP address blocks belonging to U.S. government agencies (including the Department of Defense, the Senate, and NASA), major corporations (Dell, IBM, Apple, Microsoft), and foreign governments. Traffic destined for those addresses was briefly rerouted through Chinese infrastructure before reaching its destination. The incident was formally studied by the U.S.-China Economic and Security Review Commission. Whether it was a misconfiguration or an intentional collection operation was never definitively determined — and the ambiguity is itself analytically significant.

WarningIntelligence Implications of Routing Vulnerabilities

BGP hijacking — whether accidental or deliberate — has two distinct intelligence implications. First, traffic interception: rerouting packets through a third-party network creates an opportunity to copy data in transit. Even encrypted traffic reveals metadata (source, destination, volume, timing) that is analytically valuable. Second, attribution difficulty: determining whether a BGP incident is a misconfiguration or a deliberate collection operation requires access to routing table data and behavioral analysis that most affected parties cannot conduct in real time. By the time an investigation concludes, the traffic has already transited the adversary’s infrastructure.

Resource Public Key Infrastructure (RPKI) — a cryptographic system for validating BGP route announcements — has been slowly deployed since the mid-2010s but remains far from universal adoption, leaving the Internet’s routing substrate substantively vulnerable.

Three Layers of Cyberspace

Martin Libicki’s foundational framework identifies three distinct layers of cyberspace, each with different vulnerabilities and control mechanisms:1

Physical Layer

Hardware, cables, satellites, routers, and physical infrastructure. Nations can physically seize, destroy, or interdict this layer. Jurisdiction is clearest here.

Syntactic Layer

Software, code, protocols, and operating systems that format, instruct, and control information flows. Vulnerabilities here enable exploitation without physical access (e.g., malware, zero-days).

Semantic Layer

The human-machine interface where information acquires meaning. Disinformation, influence operations, and psychological manipulation operate primarily at this layer. Control here is most contested and least understood.

WarningKey Principle

Control of any one layer does not confer control of the other two. A state that can physically cut a cable cannot necessarily control the software running over it, or the meanings humans attach to the information flowing through it.

TipWhy This Matters for Analysts

Libicki’s three-layer model gives analysts a structured way to ask where in cyberspace a capability, vulnerability, or operation is located — and to think about what that implies for attribution, mitigation, and escalation.

  • Physical layer operations (cable cuts, hardware interdiction) leave physical evidence, have clear geographic jurisdiction, and take time to execute. When analysts see physical-layer disruptions, they look for state actors with logistics capability and strategic motive.
  • Syntactic layer operations (malware, zero-days, protocol exploits) can be launched from anywhere, replicated at scale, and plausibly denied. Attribution relies on code analysis, infrastructure overlap, and TTP pattern-matching — probabilistic, not definitive.
  • Semantic layer operations (disinformation, influence campaigns) target human cognition and require no technical sophistication to execute. Their effects may outlast any technical disruption. Analysts cannot assess them using the same frameworks they use for physical or syntactic-layer activity.

Practically: when an incident occurs in cyberspace, the first analytical question should be which layer is being targeted? The answer determines what evidence to look for, who plausibly did it, and what the adversary’s end objective is likely to be.

Characteristics of Cyberspace

J.B. Sheldon identifies six defining characteristics of cyberspace as a domain:2

  1. Low cost of entry — barriers to participation are minimal compared to other domains
  2. Multiple actors — state and non-state actors coexist, creating attribution complexity
  3. Electromagnetic spectrum dependency — wireless communications can be jammed, intercepted, or denied
  4. Man-made existence — cyberspace requires physical infrastructure; it can be built, degraded, or destroyed
  5. Constant replicability — information can be copied infinitely at near-zero cost
  6. Near-instantaneous speed — effects propagate globally within milliseconds
TipWhy This Matters for Analysts

Sheldon’s six characteristics translate directly into analytical tradecraft decisions.

Low cost of entry + multiple actors means analysts cannot assume that sophisticated effects imply a sophisticated actor. A motivated individual with commodity tools can produce effects that previously required state resources. Threat attribution must resist anchoring on capability alone.

Electromagnetic spectrum dependency introduces a physical chokepoint even in a “virtual” domain. Analysts working on denied-area problems should ask whether an adversary can exploit jamming, interception, or spectrum denial to limit an opponent’s options before any kinetic engagement.

Man-made existence means cyberspace is geographically and politically bounded in ways that are invisible on a standard map. The logical topology of the Internet (which AS paths connect which countries, where traffic physically flows) is as important as physical maps for understanding reach, dependency, and vulnerability.

Constant replicability undermines traditional source-protection logic. A document leaked once is leaked permanently — there is no analogue to recovering a physical asset. Analysts handling sensitive-source material should assume that any digital copy has infinite potential distribution.

Near-instantaneous speed compresses the intelligence cycle in ways that human institutions were not designed to accommodate. Early warning systems and decision cycles built around days or hours of lead time may be operationally irrelevant in a cyber-enabled conflict. Analysts should be explicit about whether their assessments were designed to drive real-time operational decisions or longer-horizon policy ones.

The Computing Timeline Extended — 1991 to Present

From the Web to the AI Era

The milestones from 1942 to 1989 established the foundations. The decades that followed transformed those foundations into the global information environment that intelligence analysts operate in today.

1991 — World Wide Web Goes Public
Tim Berners-Lee released the first website at CERN on August 6, 1991. Within two years, web traffic was doubling every three months. The public Internet had arrived.
1993 — Mosaic Browser
The Mosaic browser introduced images, clickable links, and forms to the web, making it accessible to non-technical users. It triggered the first wave of mass-market Internet adoption and laid the groundwork for e-commerce, online media, and — eventually — large-scale OSINT collection.
1998 — Google Search
Larry Page and Sergey Brin launched Google, applying PageRank — a mathematical measure of link authority — to web indexing. Google’s model of ranking by inbound links made the open web far more navigable and accelerated the utility of OSINT. Google’s advertising revenue model also established the template for surveillance capitalism: free services funded by user data monetization.
2001 — Wikipedia
The launch of Wikipedia represented the first large-scale collaborative knowledge infrastructure on the web. By 2010 it had over 3 million English articles. It also became a target for information operations — state and non-state actors have repeatedly edited Wikipedia entries as part of influence campaigns.
2004–2006 — Social Media Platforms
Facebook launched in 2004, YouTube in 2005, Twitter in 2006. Within a decade, over half of the global Internet-connected population was voluntarily publishing location data, social connections, behavioral patterns, and opinions at massive scale — the richest open-source intelligence environment in history.
2007 — iPhone and the Mobile Internet
Apple’s iPhone merged telecommunications, computing, and GPS into a single always-connected device. Smartphone proliferation meant that billions of individuals became persistent location beacons, generating metadata at a scale unimaginable to earlier SIGINT planners.
2013 — Snowden Disclosures
Edward Snowden’s release of NSA documents revealed the scale of signals intelligence collection: PRISM (collecting data from major tech platforms), XKEYSCORE (indexing global Internet traffic), and MUSCULAR (tapping undersea cables). The disclosures demonstrated both the capabilities enabled by Internet infrastructure and the legal and political limits on their use.
2016 — AlphaGo
DeepMind’s AlphaGo defeated world champion Go player Lee Sedol 4–1, widely interpreted as a landmark in AI capability. Unlike chess engines, AlphaGo used deep reinforcement learning and was not explicitly programmed with game rules — it learned by playing itself millions of times.
2017 — Project Maven (DoD AI Initiative)
The U.S. Department of Defense awarded Google a contract to apply computer vision and machine learning to drone footage analysis — automatically identifying objects and persons of interest in video streams. It was the first major public acknowledgment that the military was systematically applying commercial AI to intelligence tasks.
2022 — ChatGPT and the Generative AI Era
OpenAI’s release of ChatGPT reached one million users in five days. It demonstrated that LLMs could engage in fluent natural language dialogue, code generation, and document summarization at a quality approaching expert human level. Within months, intelligence agencies, militaries, and adversarial actors alike were evaluating the technology’s implications.
2023–Present — Multimodal/Agentic AI and Autonomous Systems
Models capable of processing text, images, audio, and video simultaneously (GPT-4V, Gemini, Claude) raised new questions about the automation of intelligence work and the weaponization of synthetic media. Autonomous drone swarms, AI-assisted targeting, and AI-generated disinformation became operational realities in conflict zones.
TipWhy This Matters for Analysts

This timeline is not a history lesson — it is a map of the information environment analysts operate in today. Each milestone reshapes what evidence exists, where it lives, who can access it, and how it can be exploited.

Mass social media adoption (2004–2006) created the OSINT landscape. Adversaries, civilians, and allied partners all generate social footprints. An analyst who understands why Facebook’s engagement model amplifies outrage, or why Twitter’s reply graph exposes social network structure, can exploit those platforms more effectively and assess their limitations more honestly.

Smartphone proliferation (2007) means that virtually every individual in a connected society is generating persistent geolocation metadata. SIGINT and OSINT convergence is a direct consequence: commercially available location data from ad brokers now overlaps with what previously required court orders or foreign intelligence access.

The Snowden disclosures (2013) are not just a policy story. They documented the architecture of large-scale digital collection — the choke points, the legal authorities, the technical methods. Analysts who understand XKEYSCORE and PRISM understand what kinds of collection are possible and what their legal and operational constraints are.

Project Maven (2017) signals that machine learning is now embedded in the intelligence enterprise. Analysts should expect AI-assisted tools for image exploitation, pattern-of-life analysis, and document triage to become standard — and should develop enough technical literacy to understand what those tools can and cannot do.

Generative AI (2022–present) changes the threat environment as much as the analytical toolkit. Adversaries have access to the same LLMs. Synthetic text, images, and audio are now operationally affordable. Analysts assessing information reliability must treat AI-generated content as a standing hypothesis, not a remote possibility.

Part III: Data, Information, and Knowledge

The Data–Information–Knowledge Hierarchy

One of the most fundamental distinctions in intelligence analysis is understanding what separates raw data from actionable knowledge. These three concepts form a hierarchy, and movement up that hierarchy requires human (or computational) effort.

Data
Any raw, unprocessed facts or figures in organized or unorganized form. Data has no inherent meaning on its own—it requires context. Examples: a series of GPS coordinates, a list of phone numbers, a table of export quantities.
Information
Data that has been processed, organized, or structured to give it meaning. Information answers questions like who, what, where, and when. Example: a satellite image showing vehicle movement at a previously inactive military installation.
Knowledge
Information interpreted through the lens of prior experience, judgment, and context. Knowledge enables decision-making. It answers why and so what. Example: understanding that observed vehicle movement at the installation represents a pattern consistent with pre-exercise staging, not offensive preparation.
NoteIntelligence Cycle Connection

The data→information→knowledge progression mirrors stages of the intelligence cycle. Collection produces data; processing and exploitation convert it to information; analysis and production yield knowledge that informs policy decisions.

Part IV: The Intelligence Cycle

Why the Cycle Matters

Before examining how intelligence is collected, it is important to understand the process within which collection occurs. The intelligence cycle is the iterative process by which the Intelligence Community converts raw information into finished intelligence products that support national security decisions.

NoteNot a Perfect Cycle

The intelligence cycle is a teaching model, not a description of how intelligence actually flows in practice. In reality, stages overlap, run in parallel, and iterate continuously. Collection priorities shift mid-cycle based on emerging requirements. Analysts may loop back to collection because initial data is insufficient. Think of it as a framework for understanding functions, not a rigid sequence.

The Five Stages

  1. Planning and Direction
    Policymakers, commanders, and other consumers of intelligence identify their decision needs and communicate them to the IC as intelligence requirements. These requirements are prioritized and translated into collection requirements — specific questions that collection systems and human sources are tasked to answer. The outputs of this stage are collection plans and tasking orders. Example: the National Intelligence Priorities Framework (NIPF) ranks collection priorities across intelligence topics and countries.
  2. Collection
    Raw information is gathered from all available sources — HUMINT, SIGINT, IMINT, OSINT, MASINT, CYBERINT, GEOINT — in accordance with the collection plan. Collection activities may be clandestine (covert technical collection, recruited human sources) or overt (embassy reporting, open-source monitoring). Not all collection is successful; gaps are common.
  3. Processing and Exploitation
    Raw collected data is converted into a form usable by analysts. This stage is often the most technically intensive: intercepted signals must be decrypted and transcribed; satellite imagery must be orthorectified and georeferenced; documents in foreign languages must be translated; terabytes of signals data must be filtered for relevant content. Many IC agencies have dedicated exploitation centers for each collection discipline.
  4. Analysis and Production
    Processed information is integrated, evaluated, interpreted, and converted into finished intelligence products — assessments, estimates, briefings, and warnings. This is where the data–information–knowledge transition fully occurs. Analysts apply structured analytic techniques (SATs), consult with subject matter experts, and coordinate across agencies to produce products that convey confidence levels and acknowledge uncertainty.
  5. Dissemination
    Finished intelligence products are delivered to policymakers, commanders, and other consumers. Products are tailored to the consumer’s needs: the President’s Daily Brief (PDB) is concise and action-oriented; a National Intelligence Estimate (NIE) is comprehensive and methodologically detailed. Feedback from consumers about what was useful (or not) loops back to Planning and Direction, starting the cycle again.

Hulnick, A. S. (2006). What’s wrong with the intelligence cycle. Intelligence and National Security, 21(6), 959–979. Via the JMU Library

Critiques the Intelligence Cycle as a model — argues it misrepresents how intelligence actually works, particularly the relationship between collection and analysis. Reading this alongside the standard cycle model is essential for developing a critical perspective on the framework.
