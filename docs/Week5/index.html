Week 5:Probability and Uncertainty in Analysis

IA 240: Technology Applications in a Networked World

Intelligence analysis is reasoning under uncertainty. This lecture formalizes that reasoning with probability theory — covering classical and Bayesian probability, the rules of probability, and how explicit probabilistic reasoning corrects systematic cognitive errors that distort analytical judgment. The probabilistic language standards used by the Intelligence Community are grounded in the formal framework introduced here.

Part I: What is Probability?

Defining Probability

Probability is a mathematical framework for quantifying uncertainty. It gives analysts a rigorous, communicable language for expressing the likelihood of events—from coin flips to geopolitical crises.

The classical definition:

\[P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of Favorable Outcomes}}{\text{Total Number of Possible Outcomes}}\]

where \(P(A)\) is the probability that event \(A\) occurs, constrained to the range \([0, 1]\):

  • \(P(A) = 0\): event is impossible
  • \(P(A) = 1\): event is certain
  • \(P(A) = 0.5\): equally likely to occur or not occur
NoteWhy This Matters for Intelligence Analysis

Intelligence analysis is fundamentally about reasoning under uncertainty. Explicit probabilistic language—“we assess with high confidence (>85%) that…”—is more precise and less manipulable than vague hedges like “likely” or “probably.” The Intelligence Community uses standardized probability ranges for exactly this reason.

Sample Space

The sample space (\(S\) or \(\Omega\)) is the set of all possible outcomes for a given experiment or scenario.

Properties: - Must be exhaustive—every possible outcome is represented - Outcomes must be mutually exclusive—no two can occur simultaneously

Experiment Sample Space
Roll a six-sided die \(\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}\)
Flip a coin \(\{H, T\}\)
A country holds an election: incumbent wins or loses \(\{\text{Win}, \text{Loss}\}\)
A state either acquires nuclear weapons or does not within 5 years \(\{\text{Acquire}, \neg\text{Acquire}\}\)

Outcomes: Simple and Compound

Simple Outcome
A single point in the sample space. Example: rolling a “3” on a die.
Compound Outcome
A subset of the sample space containing more than one point. Example: rolling an odd number (1, 3, or 5). In intelligence terms: “a conflict occurs” might encompass multiple distinct pathways (border skirmish, cyber attack, proxy conflict).

Part II: Rules of Probability

The Addition Rule

Used to find the probability that at least one of two events occurs (A or B, or both):

\[P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)\]

The subtraction term removes double-counting of cases where both events occur simultaneously.

Example: Drawing a card from a standard 52-card deck — probability it is a King or a Heart:

\[P(\text{King} \cup \text{Heart}) = \frac{4}{52} + \frac{13}{52} - \frac{1}{52} = \frac{16}{52} = \frac{4}{13} \approx 0.308\]

TipSpecial Case: Mutually Exclusive Events

If \(A\) and \(B\) cannot both occur (\(P(A \cap B) = 0\)), the addition rule simplifies to \(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)\).

The Multiplication Rule

Used to find the probability that both event A and event B occur:

\[P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A)\]

where \(P(B \mid A)\) is the conditional probability of B given that A has occurred.

Example: Rolling a 6 then flipping heads:

\[P(6 \text{ and } H) = P(6) \cdot P(H \mid 6) = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{12}\]

WarningParlay Bets — The Multiplication Rule in Action

Sports parlays require multiple independent bets to all win. The probability compounds multiplicatively:

  • New Jersey (September 2024): parlays generated 72.5% of sportsbook gross revenue
  • Hold percentage on parlays: 24.2% vs. 4.4% for straight bets
  • Nevada (historical): 31% hold on parlays vs. 5% on standard football wagers

This is not coincidence. Every additional leg of a parlay multiplies the probability of losing. The math is transparent—and profitable for the house.

Part III: Bayesian vs. Frequentist Thinking

The Coin Flip Thought Experiment

Consider a fair coin. Before the flip, \(P(\text{Heads}) = 0.5\). Now imagine I flip the coin, cover it with my hand, and ask: “What is the probability it is heads?”

  • For a frequentist, the coin is already determined—the question has an unknown but fixed answer; probability, in the formal sense, does not apply to this realized event.
  • For a Bayesian, your probability (0.5) represents your epistemic state—your uncertainty about the outcome. If someone else peeked under my hand, their probability would be 0 or 1. Probability describes knowledge, not just the world.

The key insight: probability describes our knowledge about the world, not solely objective features of the world itself.

Comparing the Two Frameworks

Frequentist

  • Probability = long-run relative frequency of outcomes over repeated trials
  • The parameter being estimated is fixed (not a random variable)
  • Cannot assign probability to hypotheses directly
  • Computes: “If the null hypothesis were true, how likely is data this extreme?” → the p-value
  • Never directly tells you whether the hypothesis is true

Bayesian

  • Probability = degree of rational belief, updated as evidence accumulates
  • Parameters are random variables (can have probability distributions)
  • Can compute \(P(\text{Hypothesis} \mid \text{Evidence})\) directly
  • Requires specifying a prior (belief before evidence)
  • Updates priors to posteriors using observed data
NoteWhich Approach Is “Right”?

Both frameworks have strong theoretical and practical justifications. Frequentist methods dominate classical statistics and much of published social science. Bayesian methods are increasingly prominent in intelligence analysis, medicine, and machine learning—wherever incorporating prior knowledge explicitly is desirable and updating beliefs as evidence accumulates is the primary goal.

Applied Example: Finding the Phone

You’ve misplaced your phone somewhere in your home. Pressing the base station locator causes the phone to beep—but the sound is faint and muffled.

  • Prior distribution: your initial beliefs about where the phone is, based on where you usually leave it (desk, bedroom, kitchen)
  • Evidence: the direction and volume of the beep narrows down the possibilities
  • Posterior: your updated beliefs about the phone’s location given the beep

This is exactly how Bayesian reasoning works: you do not start from scratch with each piece of evidence—you update your existing beliefs.

Part IV: Bayes’ Theorem

The Formula

Bayes’ Theorem formalizes how to update probabilities given new evidence:

\[P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}\]

In intelligence notation, using Hypothesis (H) and Evidence (E):

\[P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)}\]

\(P(H)\) — Prior
Your probability for the hypothesis before observing the evidence. Represents existing knowledge, base rates, or initial belief.
\(P(H \mid E)\) — Posterior
Your updated probability for the hypothesis after observing the evidence. This is what you want.
\(P(E \mid H)\) — Likelihood
The probability of observing this evidence if the hypothesis were true. How consistent is this evidence with the hypothesis?
\(P(E)\) — Marginal Probability of Evidence
The total probability of observing this evidence across all hypotheses. Acts as a normalizing constant.

When to Use Bayes’ Theorem

Apply Bayesian reasoning when you have:

  1. A hypothesis (an uncertain claim about the world)
  2. Evidence (new observations)
  3. A desire to estimate \(P(\text{Hypothesis} \mid \text{Evidence})\)

Worked Example 1: Librarian or Farmer?

Steve is shy, withdrawn, tidy, and has a passion for detail. Is he more likely a librarian or a farmer?1

Most people intuitively say “librarian”—but the base rate matters. In the United States, there are roughly 200 farmers for every librarian. Even if the description fits librarians far better than farmers on average, the overwhelming numerical advantage of farmers dominates.

Using Bayes:

  • Prior \(P(\text{Librarian}) = \frac{1}{201} \approx 0.005\); \(P(\text{Farmer}) = \frac{200}{201} \approx 0.995\)
  • After applying a likelihood ratio that the description fits librarians ~40× better than farmers:
    • \(P(\text{Librarian} \mid \text{Description}) \approx 17\%\)
    • \(P(\text{Farmer} \mid \text{Description}) \approx 83\%\)

The lesson: base rates are evidence. Ignoring them (base rate neglect) is a well-documented cognitive bias that Bayesian reasoning directly corrects.

Worked Example 2: Fire and Smoke

Given: - Dangerous fires are rare: \(P(\text{Fire}) = 0.01\) - Smoke is fairly common: \(P(\text{Smoke}) = 0.10\) (due to barbecues, etc.) - Fires almost always produce smoke: \(P(\text{Smoke} \mid \text{Fire}) = 0.90\)

Question: \(P(\text{Fire} \mid \text{Smoke}) = ?\)

Solution: \[P(\text{Fire} \mid \text{Smoke}) = \frac{P(\text{Smoke} \mid \text{Fire}) \cdot P(\text{Fire})}{P(\text{Smoke})}\] \[= \frac{0.90 \times 0.01}{0.10} = \frac{0.009}{0.10} = 0.09\]

Even with smoke present, there is only a 9% probability of a dangerous fire. This reflects the power of low base rates.

The Core Principle

Evidence should not determine beliefs—it should update them.

Look at all cases where the evidence is true. What proportion of those cases also feature the hypothesis? That proportion is your posterior.

Part V: Application to Intelligence Analysis

The Intelligence Context

NoteA Recent Example

Scenario: Concern has risen following Russian drone incursions into Polish airspace.

Frequentist framing: If Russia had no intent to escalate against Poland, what is the probability we would observe drone incursions at this frequency? (Tests consistency of evidence with a null hypothesis.)

Bayesian framing: Given our prior belief about the probability of Russian hostile action against Poland, how should our belief change after observing the drone incursions? (Updates beliefs in light of evidence.)

Both framings are useful. The Bayesian framing more naturally incorporates what we already knew; the frequentist framing is useful for hypothesis rejection under formal uncertainty.

WarningCommon Analytical Failures Addressed by Bayesian Thinking
  • Ignoring base rates — overweighting vivid evidence and underweighting background frequencies
  • Confirmation bias — only updating in response to confirming evidence; discounting disconfirming evidence
  • Anchoring — being insufficiently responsive to new evidence because the prior is too strong
  • Representativeness heuristic — judging likelihood by how “typical” something looks, rather than by actual probabilities (the Steve/librarian problem)

Suggested Readings

All readings are journal or news articles.

Jervis, R. (2006). Reports, politics, and intelligence failures: The case of Iraq. Journal of Strategic Studies, 29(1), 3–52. https://doi.org/10.1080/01402390600566282

Post-mortem analysis of the 2002 Iraq WMD assessment. The cognitive biases the Bayesian framework is designed to correct — base rate neglect, confirmation bias, anchoring — appear throughout this case. Reading the theory and the failure case together is essential.

Bar-Joseph, U., & Kruglanski, A. W. (2003). Intelligence failure and need for cognitive closure. Political Psychology, 24(1), 75–99. https://doi.org/10.1111/0162-895X.00320

Examines how analysts’ psychological need for certainty leads to premature closure on hypotheses and resistance to disconfirming evidence — exactly what Bayesian reasoning disciplines. Applied to the Yom Kippur War intelligence failure.

Tetlock, P. E. (1999). Theory-driven reasoning about plausible pasts and probable futures in world politics. American Journal of Political Science, 43(2), 335–366. https://doi.org/10.2307/2991799

Provides empirical evidence that analysts with more probabilistic, self-correcting reasoning styles outperform those with committed theoretical frameworks on forecasting tasks.

Van der Bles, A. M., van der Linden, S., Freeman, A. L. J., Mitchell, J., Galvao, A. B., Zaval, L., & Spiegelhalter, D. J. (2019). Communicating uncertainty about facts, numbers and science. Royal Society Open Science, 6(5), 181870. https://doi.org/10.1098/rsos.181870

Empirically examines how different methods of communicating uncertainty (numerical ranges, verbal qualifiers, visual representations) affect consumer understanding. Directly relevant to the ICD 203 standardized probability language conventions.
